## COPY (artículo)

**H1:** When Should You Take Senior Pictures?

**H2: The Short Answer**

Most seniors take their pictures the **summer before senior year or in early fall**, ahead of yearbook deadlines. But after twenty years of photographing seniors in the Tri-Cities, my honest answer is: the best time is the one that matches *your* season — and it's almost never actually "too late."

**H2: When Are You Supposed to Take Senior Pictures?**

There's no official rule — just a practical one: your yearbook deadline. Most Tri-Cities high schools ask for senior portraits sometime in the fall of senior year `[VALIDAR: fechas concretas de los distritos de Richland, Kennewick y Pasco — dato local que nadie más publica]`. Working backward from that deadline, a summer or early-fall session gives you time to shoot, choose your favorites, and submit without stress.

Beyond the yearbook, families time sessions around sports seasons, hair appointments, braces coming off, and — smart ones — the weather they love most.

**H2: Season by Season: What Each One Gives You**

**H3: Spring of junior year**
Blossoms, green fields and soft light — and you beat the senior-year rush entirely. Choosing spring means your photos are done before applications, sports and lasts-of-everything take over.

**H3: Summer before senior year**
The most popular window, for good reason: long golden evenings, tan lines optional, no school-schedule conflicts. In the Tri-Cities, summer golden hour is generous — warm light that lasts and lasts. Book early; these dates go first.

**H3: Early fall of senior year**
October along the river is the Tri-Cities showing off — autumn color, honey light, sweater weather. It's also yearbook-deadline season, so fall sessions book out fastest of all.

**H3: Winter**
Underrated. Clean light, quiet locations, moody skies — and a calendar with far more open dates. If your style leans dramatic or cozy, winter might be your season.

**H2: When Is It Too Late to Take Senior Pictures?**

Here's the answer most websites won't give you: **it's only too late for the yearbook — never for the photographs.** If the yearbook deadline has passed, submit whatever photo the school needs and then take your real session whenever it works. Senior pictures aren't for the yearbook page; they're for you, your parents, your graduation announcements — and the version of you that only existed this year. I've photographed wonderful senior sessions in February, in April, even after graduation. Those photos matter just as much.

**H2: What's the Best Time of Day for Senior Pictures Outside?**

Golden hour — roughly the last 60 to 90 minutes before sunset. The light turns warm and directional, skin looks its best, and backgrounds glow instead of glare. In the Tri-Cities that means an evening session almost year-round; exact timing shifts with the season, and I plan it to the minute for every shoot.

**H2: The Tri-Cities Booking Calendar (What Nobody Tells You)**

Senior dates fill faster than anything else I photograph. My practical advice, learned from twenty years of local booking patterns: reserve your session **four to six weeks before** the date you want, and earlier for the golden windows — summer evenings and October. If you're reading this in spring wanting a fall session, now is exactly the right time to reach out.

**H2: Quick Answers**

- **Junior or senior year?** Either. Spring junior year or summer/fall senior year are both ideal.
- **Before or after getting braces off?** After, if the timing is close — or we celebrate the smile you have now. Your call.
- **Can I do more than one season?** Yes — some seniors split outfits across two mini-dates for variety. `[VALIDAR: si Lisa ofrece esto — Q54]`
- **Is winter too cold?** We plan around it: shorter sets, warm layers between shots, and light that's worth it.

**H2: Whenever You Shoot, Make It Yours**

The timeline matters less than this: your senior pictures should look like *you* — your style, your places, your story. That's what we plan together, and it's the part I've loved for twenty years.
